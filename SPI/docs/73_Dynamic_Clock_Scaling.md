# 73. SPI Dynamic Clock Scaling

**Background** — how SPI clocks are derived from a peripheral bus clock via power-of-two prescalers, and the slave-device constraints (`f_max`, `f_min`, setup/hold times) that bound the usable range.

**Why DCS?** — a table of seven motivations: power/thermal savings, EMI reduction, multi-slave buses with different limits, supply-voltage tracking, and DVFS integration.

**Three core scaling policies** are implemented in both languages:
- *Workload-driven* — burst vs. background transfers (C with STM32 HAL + direct register manipulation)
- *Thermal-driven* — a de-rating lookup table (C++ template class; Rust generic struct with full unit tests)
- *Voltage-tracking* — capping `f_max` as the battery depletes (C lookup + Rust struct)

A **combined multi-policy scaler** (Rust) applies all three simultaneously and returns the most conservative result. A **`DynamicSpiClock` trait** cleanly separates policy from hardware, with a `MockSpi` stub for test-driven development.

**Advanced patterns** include hysteresis to prevent clock thrashing, a clock ramp-up/down helper, and the Linux `spi_transfer.speed_hz` per-transfer override pattern.

The **summary** distils the 10–30 % battery-life improvement potential and the key rules: always wait for BSY to clear, clamp to per-slave limits, and use hysteresis.

## Introduction

**Dynamic Clock Scaling (DCS)** in the context of SPI (Serial Peripheral Interface) refers to
the runtime adjustment of the SPI bus clock frequency (SCLK) to balance trade-offs between
power consumption, electromagnetic interference (EMI), thermal output, and data throughput.
Rather than operating at a fixed clock rate determined at compile time or system initialisation,
a DCS-enabled driver continuously or event-driven re-programmes the SPI peripheral's baud-rate
register to match the current workload and power budget.

SPI is a synchronous, full-duplex serial protocol. Its clock is always generated by the master.
The maximum permissible rate is constrained by the slowest device on the bus, PCB trace lengths,
and the supply voltage. Within those hard limits there is often a wide, exploitable range –
sometimes spanning two or three orders of magnitude – that DCS can leverage.

---

## Background: SPI Clock Fundamentals

### Clock Generation

Most microcontrollers derive the SPI clock from a peripheral bus clock (PCLK) via a prescaler
divider:

```
SCLK = PCLK / (2 × prescaler)    (common formula; varies by vendor)
```

Typical prescaler values are powers of two: 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64, 128, 256.

On more capable SoCs (e.g. STM32H7, i.MX RT, RP2040) a fractional or PLL-based divider gives
finer-grained control.

### SPI Modes and Timing

Clock polarity (CPOL) and phase (CPHA) define the four SPI modes (0–3). These are **independent**
of the clock frequency, so mode settings need not change when scaling the clock.

### Slave Device Constraints

Every SPI slave has:
- `f_max`  – absolute maximum clock frequency
- `f_min`  – minimum clock for reliable operation (e.g. some Flash memories require ≥ 1 MHz)
- `t_setup`, `t_hold` – setup and hold times relative to SCLK edges

At lower voltages (e.g. in under-voltage conditions) `f_max` typically decreases, which is
one of the inputs a DCS system must respect.

---

## Why Dynamic Clock Scaling?

| Motivation | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Power saving** | Dynamic power in CMOS scales as `P ∝ C × V² × f`. Halving the SPI clock roughly halves the switching power of the peripheral and any line-termination resistors. |
| **Thermal management** | Lower frequency → lower power → lower junction temperature. |
| **EMI / signal integrity** | High-frequency edges radiate more. Slowing the clock during non-critical transfers reduces EMI. |
| **Multi-slave buses** | Different slaves may have very different `f_max`. Scaling per-transaction avoids over-clocking a slow device. |
| **Supply-voltage tracking** | When the supply droops (e.g. battery end-of-life), `f_max` of Flash and ADC slaves decreases. DCS adapts. |
| **Thermal throttling** | If a SoC temperature sensor reports overheating, reducing peripheral clock rates sheds power quickly. |
| **OS / DVFS integration** | Many RTOS and Linux power-management frameworks model peripheral clocks as part of the overall DVFS (Dynamic Voltage and Frequency Scaling) policy. |

---

## Clock Scaling Strategies

### 1. Workload-Driven Scaling

Scale up during burst transfers, scale down during idle or low-priority background transfers.

```
Idle ──(burst request)──► High clock ──(burst done)──► Cooldown ──► Low clock
```

### 2. Temperature-Driven Scaling

A thermal sensor feeds a PID or lookup-table controller that adjusts SPI (and other peripheral)
clocks to stay within a thermal envelope.

### 3. Battery / Supply-Voltage Tracking

An ADC monitors Vcc or Vbat. As the voltage falls below thresholds, the maximum allowed clock
is stepped down.

### 4. Per-Slave Profiles

A table stores the `f_max` for each chip-select. The driver programs the lowest clock that
satisfies the current transaction's required throughput, capped at that slave's `f_max`.

### 5. Feedback / Adaptive Scaling

Measure transfer error rates (CRC failures, NACK-equivalents). If errors increase when the
clock is raised, back off and record a new safe ceiling for that slave.

---

## Hardware Considerations

### Propagation Delay and Trace Length

At high SCLK frequencies, PCB trace impedance and propagation delay matter. A rule of thumb:
keep traces shorter than λ/10 at the highest clock harmonic. For 50 MHz SCLK the third harmonic
is 150 MHz → λ ≈ 2 m in FR4 → keep traces < 20 cm. Dynamically reducing the clock relaxes this
constraint.

### Clock Re-configuration Timing

The SPI peripheral must be **idle** (no active transfer) when reprogramming the prescaler.
Most hardware requires the peripheral to be disabled, reconfigured, then re-enabled. This
introduces a small latency (microseconds) which must be accounted for in real-time systems.

### Clock Glitching

Some prescaler implementations may produce a partial clock pulse during a mid-transfer
reconfiguration. Always wait for the BSY (busy) flag to clear and ensure CS is de-asserted
before changing the clock.

### 2. STM32 HAL – Concrete Clock Scaling Implementation

```c
/* spi_dcs_stm32.c
 * Demonstrates dynamic SPI clock scaling on STM32 using HAL.
 * Tested concept on STM32F4 / STM32H7 families.
 */

#include "stm32f4xx_hal.h"   /* adapt to your target */
#include "spi_dcs.h"
#include <math.h>

/* -----------------------------------------------------------------------
 * Internal helpers
 * --------------------------------------------------------------------- */

/** Map a desired clock to the nearest achievable prescaler value.
 *  STM32 SPI prescaler register: 0=div2, 1=div4, ... 7=div256 */
static uint32_t _best_prescaler(uint32_t pclk_hz, uint32_t target_hz)
{
    /* Iterate from the smallest (fastest) prescaler upward */
    for (uint8_t p = 0; p <= 7; p++) {
        uint32_t achieved = pclk_hz / (1u << (p + 1));
        if (achieved <= target_hz) {
            return p;   /* SPI_BAUDRATEPRESCALER_2 << p */
        }
    }
    return 7; /* div256 – slowest */
}

/** Convert prescaler index to HAL constant */
static uint32_t _prescaler_to_hal(uint32_t p)
{
    /* STM32 HAL constants are already encoded as (p << 3) */
    static const uint32_t hal_table[] = {
        SPI_BAUDRATEPRESCALER_2,
        SPI_BAUDRATEPRESCALER_4,
        SPI_BAUDRATEPRESCALER_8,
        SPI_BAUDRATEPRESCALER_16,
        SPI_BAUDRATEPRESCALER_32,
        SPI_BAUDRATEPRESCALER_64,
        SPI_BAUDRATEPRESCALER_128,
        SPI_BAUDRATEPRESCALER_256,
    };
    return hal_table[p & 0x7];
}

/* -----------------------------------------------------------------------
 * Public API Implementation
 * --------------------------------------------------------------------- */

/**
 * @brief  Reconfigure the SPI peripheral to a new clock frequency.
 *
 * This function is safe to call between transactions (CS de-asserted,
 * BSY flag clear).  It temporarily disables the peripheral, updates the
 * prescaler, and re-enables it.
 *
 * @param  hspi       STM32 HAL SPI handle
 * @param  pclk_hz    APB clock feeding the SPI peripheral
 * @param  target_hz  Desired SCLK frequency
 * @return Actual clock frequency achieved (Hz)
 */
uint32_t spi_scale_clock_stm32(SPI_HandleTypeDef *hspi,
                                uint32_t pclk_hz,
                                uint32_t target_hz)
{
    /* 1. Wait until the peripheral is not busy */
    uint32_t timeout = 10000;
    while (__HAL_SPI_GET_FLAG(hspi, SPI_FLAG_BSY) && --timeout) { /* spin */ }
    if (timeout == 0) {
        return 0; /* Error: timeout */
    }

    /* 2. Disable the SPI peripheral */
    __HAL_SPI_DISABLE(hspi);

    /* 3. Compute the best prescaler */
    uint32_t p   = _best_prescaler(pclk_hz, target_hz);
    uint32_t hal = _prescaler_to_hal(p);

    /* 4. Update the BRR field in CR1 */
    MODIFY_REG(hspi->Instance->CR1,
               SPI_CR1_BR_Msk,
               hal);

    hspi->Init.BaudRatePrescaler = hal;

    /* 5. Re-enable the peripheral */
    __HAL_SPI_ENABLE(hspi);

    /* 6. Return the achieved frequency */
    uint32_t achieved = pclk_hz / (1u << (p + 1));
    return achieved;
}

/* -----------------------------------------------------------------------
 * Workload-driven policy example
 * --------------------------------------------------------------------- */

#define SMALL_TRANSFER_THRESHOLD   32u    /* bytes */
#define PCLK_HZ                    90000000u

typedef struct {
    SPI_HandleTypeDef *hspi;
    spi_slave_cfg_t    slave;
    uint32_t           pclk_hz;
    uint32_t           current_hz;
} spi_dcs_ctx_t;

/**
 * @brief  Choose an appropriate clock for the coming transfer.
 *
 * Policy: use the low-power clock for small, non-urgent transfers;
 *         use the preferred clock for large or urgent transfers.
 */
uint32_t spi_dcs_select_workload_clock(const spi_dcs_ctx_t *ctx,
                                        size_t transfer_bytes,
                                        bool   urgent)
{
    uint32_t desired;

    if (urgent || transfer_bytes > SMALL_TRANSFER_THRESHOLD) {
        desired = ctx->slave.f_preferred_hz;
    } else {
        desired = ctx->slave.f_low_power_hz;
    }

    /* Clamp to slave limits */
    if (desired > ctx->slave.f_max_hz) desired = ctx->slave.f_max_hz;
    if (desired < ctx->slave.f_min_hz) desired = ctx->slave.f_min_hz;

    return desired;
}

/**
 * @brief  Execute a DCS-aware SPI transaction.
 */
HAL_StatusTypeDef spi_dcs_transact(spi_dcs_ctx_t  *ctx,
                                    uint8_t        *tx_buf,
                                    uint8_t        *rx_buf,
                                    size_t          len,
                                    bool            urgent)
{
    /* 1. Determine and apply the appropriate clock */
    uint32_t desired_hz = spi_dcs_select_workload_clock(ctx, len, urgent);

    if (desired_hz != ctx->current_hz) {
        ctx->current_hz = spi_scale_clock_stm32(ctx->hspi,
                                                  ctx->pclk_hz,
                                                  desired_hz);
    }

    /* 2. Assert chip select, perform transfer, de-assert */
    HAL_GPIO_WritePin(GPIOA, ctx->slave.cs_pin, GPIO_PIN_RESET);
    HAL_StatusTypeDef status = HAL_SPI_TransmitReceive(
            ctx->hspi, tx_buf, rx_buf, (uint16_t)len, HAL_MAX_DELAY);
    HAL_GPIO_WritePin(GPIOA, ctx->slave.cs_pin, GPIO_PIN_SET);

    return status;
}
```

### 3. Thermal-Driven Scaling (C++)

```cpp
// thermal_spi_scaler.hpp
// C++17 – Temperature-feedback dynamic clock scaler

#pragma once
#include <cstdint>
#include <algorithm>
#include <array>

/**
 * @brief  Thermal look-up table entry.
 *
 * Maps a temperature threshold to the maximum allowed SPI clock.
 * Table must be sorted by temp_c ascending.
 */
struct ThermalClockEntry {
    float    temp_c;       ///< Temperature threshold (°C)
    uint32_t max_clk_hz;   ///< Maximum SPI clock at or below this temp
};

/**
 * @brief  Determines the maximum SPI clock given the current temperature.
 *
 * @tparam N   Number of entries in the thermal table
 */
template<std::size_t N>
class ThermalSpiScaler {
public:
    explicit ThermalSpiScaler(
            const std::array<ThermalClockEntry, N>& table,
            uint32_t absolute_max_hz,
            uint32_t absolute_min_hz)
        : table_(table),
          abs_max_hz_(absolute_max_hz),
          abs_min_hz_(absolute_min_hz)
    {}

    /**
     * @brief  Compute the clock ceiling for a given temperature.
     * @param  temp_c   Current SoC / board temperature in degrees Celsius
     * @return Maximum allowable SPI clock frequency (Hz)
     */
    [[nodiscard]]
    uint32_t max_clock_for_temp(float temp_c) const noexcept
    {
        uint32_t ceiling = abs_max_hz_;

        for (const auto& entry : table_) {
            if (temp_c <= entry.temp_c) {
                ceiling = entry.max_clk_hz;
                break;
            }
        }

        return std::clamp(ceiling, abs_min_hz_, abs_max_hz_);
    }

    /**
     * @brief  Choose an SPI clock that meets both a desired throughput
     *         AND the thermal ceiling.
     *
     * @param  desired_hz   Throughput-driven desired clock
     * @param  temp_c       Current temperature
     * @return Recommended clock frequency
     */
    [[nodiscard]]
    uint32_t recommend_clock(uint32_t desired_hz, float temp_c) const noexcept
    {
        uint32_t thermal_ceiling = max_clock_for_temp(temp_c);
        return std::min(desired_hz, thermal_ceiling);
    }

private:
    std::array<ThermalClockEntry, N> table_;
    uint32_t abs_max_hz_;
    uint32_t abs_min_hz_;
};


// -------------------------------------------------------------------------
// Usage example
// -------------------------------------------------------------------------

#include <cstdio>

void thermal_scaler_demo()
{
    constexpr std::array<ThermalClockEntry, 4> thermal_table = {{
        {  60.0f,  40'000'000u },  // Up to 60 °C:  40 MHz
        {  75.0f,  20'000'000u },  // Up to 75 °C:  20 MHz
        {  85.0f,  10'000'000u },  // Up to 85 °C:  10 MHz
        { 100.0f,   1'000'000u },  // Up to 100 °C:  1 MHz  (emergency)
    }};

    ThermalSpiScaler<4> scaler(thermal_table,
                               /*abs_max=*/ 50'000'000u,
                               /*abs_min=*/    100'000u);

    // Simulate varying temperatures
    const float temps[]   = { 45.0f, 62.0f, 78.0f, 88.0f };
    const uint32_t desired = 40'000'000u;

    for (float t : temps) {
        uint32_t clk = scaler.recommend_clock(desired, t);
        printf("Temp: %5.1f°C  →  SPI clock: %8u Hz\n", (double)t, clk);
    }
}

/*  Output:
    Temp:  45.0°C  →  SPI clock: 40000000 Hz
    Temp:  62.0°C  →  SPI clock: 40000000 Hz
    Temp:  78.0°C  →  SPI clock: 20000000 Hz
    Temp:  88.0°C  →  SPI clock: 10000000 Hz
*/
```

### 4. Voltage-Tracking Scaler (C)

```c
/* voltage_spi_scaler.c
 * Scale SPI clock with falling supply voltage.
 * Suitable for battery-powered devices.
 */

#include <stdint.h>

typedef struct {
    uint32_t voltage_mv;   /* Supply voltage threshold (mV) */
    uint32_t max_clk_hz;   /* Maximum SPI clock at or above this voltage */
} voltage_clk_entry_t;

/* Table: sorted DESCENDING by voltage_mv */
static const voltage_clk_entry_t VOLTAGE_CLK_TABLE[] = {
    { 3300, 50000000u },  /* 3.3 V: full speed  */
    { 3000, 30000000u },  /* 3.0 V: reduced     */
    { 2800, 16000000u },  /* 2.8 V: conservative*/
    { 2500,  8000000u },  /* 2.5 V: low-power   */
    { 2200,  1000000u },  /* 2.2 V: emergency   */
};

#define TABLE_LEN (sizeof(VOLTAGE_CLK_TABLE) / sizeof(VOLTAGE_CLK_TABLE[0]))

/**
 * @brief  Return the maximum SPI clock for the given supply voltage.
 * @param  voltage_mv  Measured supply voltage in millivolts
 * @param  desired_hz  Desired clock (throughput-driven); will be capped
 * @return Recommended clock frequency (Hz)
 */
uint32_t voltage_spi_max_clock(uint32_t voltage_mv, uint32_t desired_hz)
{
    uint32_t ceiling = VOLTAGE_CLK_TABLE[TABLE_LEN - 1].max_clk_hz;

    for (size_t i = 0; i < TABLE_LEN; i++) {
        if (voltage_mv >= VOLTAGE_CLK_TABLE[i].voltage_mv) {
            ceiling = VOLTAGE_CLK_TABLE[i].max_clk_hz;
            break;
        }
    }

    return (desired_hz < ceiling) ? desired_hz : ceiling;
}
```

```rust
// thermal.rs  – Thermal-feedback clock scaler (no_std)

/// One entry in the thermal de-rating table.
#[derive(Debug, Clone, Copy)]
pub struct ThermalEntry {
    /// Temperature threshold in centidegrees Celsius (e.g. 7500 = 75.00 °C).
    /// Using integer arithmetic avoids a floating-point dependency.
    pub temp_cdeg: i32,
    /// Maximum SPI clock allowed at or below this temperature (Hz).
    pub max_clk_hz: u32,
}

/// Temperature-driven SPI clock scaler.
///
/// The table must be sorted in **ascending** order of `temp_cdeg`.
pub struct ThermalScaler<'a> {
    table: &'a [ThermalEntry],
    abs_max_hz: u32,
    abs_min_hz: u32,
}

impl<'a> ThermalScaler<'a> {
    /// Create a new scaler from a sorted thermal table.
    pub const fn new(
        table: &'a [ThermalEntry],
        abs_max_hz: u32,
        abs_min_hz: u32,
    ) -> Self {
        Self { table, abs_max_hz, abs_min_hz }
    }

    /// Return the maximum allowable SPI clock for `temp_cdeg`.
    ///
    /// Uses a linear scan; fine for small tables (< 16 entries).
    pub fn ceiling_hz(&self, temp_cdeg: i32) -> u32 {
        let mut ceiling = self.abs_max_hz;

        for entry in self.table {
            if temp_cdeg <= entry.temp_cdeg {
                ceiling = entry.max_clk_hz;
                break;
            }
        }

        ceiling.clamp(self.abs_min_hz, self.abs_max_hz)
    }

    /// Recommend a clock that satisfies both `desired_hz` and the
    /// thermal ceiling.
    #[inline]
    pub fn recommend(&self, desired_hz: u32, temp_cdeg: i32) -> u32 {
        desired_hz.min(self.ceiling_hz(temp_cdeg))
    }
}
```

```rust
// voltage.rs  – Supply-voltage-tracking SPI clock scaler

/// One entry in the voltage de-rating table.
#[derive(Debug, Clone, Copy)]
pub struct VoltageEntry {
    /// Voltage threshold in millivolts
    pub voltage_mv: u32,
    /// Maximum SPI clock at or above this voltage (Hz)
    pub max_clk_hz: u32,
}

/// Supply-voltage-driven SPI clock scaler.
///
/// The table must be sorted in **descending** order of `voltage_mv`.
pub struct VoltageScaler<'a> {
    table: &'a [VoltageEntry],
    abs_min_hz: u32,
}

impl<'a> VoltageScaler<'a> {
    pub const fn new(table: &'a [VoltageEntry], abs_min_hz: u32) -> Self {
        Self { table, abs_min_hz }
    }

    pub fn ceiling_hz(&self, voltage_mv: u32) -> u32 {
        // Walk the table (descending voltage order)
        for entry in self.table {
            if voltage_mv >= entry.voltage_mv {
                return entry.max_clk_hz;
            }
        }
        // Below every entry → use the minimum
        self.abs_min_hz
    }

    #[inline]
    pub fn recommend(&self, desired_hz: u32, voltage_mv: u32) -> u32 {
        desired_hz.min(self.ceiling_hz(voltage_mv))
    }
}
```

```rust
    /// Supply voltage in millivolts
    pub voltage_mv: u32,
    /// Transfer length in bytes (influences workload policy)
    pub transfer_bytes: usize,
    /// Whether this transfer is time-critical
    pub urgent: bool,
}

/// Combined policy scaler.
pub struct CombinedScaler<'a> {
    thermal: &'a ThermalScaler<'a>,
    voltage: &'a VoltageScaler<'a>,
    small_transfer_threshold: usize,
}

impl<'a> CombinedScaler<'a> {
    pub fn new(
        thermal: &'a ThermalScaler<'a>,
        voltage: &'a VoltageScaler<'a>,
        small_threshold: usize,
    ) -> Self {
        Self {
            thermal,
            voltage,
            small_transfer_threshold: small_threshold,
        }
    }

    /// Choose the best clock for `desired_hz` given `env`.
    ///
    /// Applies thermal, voltage, and workload policies and returns
    /// the minimum (most conservative) result.
    pub fn recommend(&self, desired_hz: u32, env: &EnvState) -> u32 {
        // --- Workload policy ---
        let workload_clk = if env.urgent
            || env.transfer_bytes > self.small_transfer_threshold
        {
            desired_hz          // full speed
        } else {
            desired_hz / 4      // background: quarter speed
        };

        // --- Thermal policy ---
        let thermal_clk = self.thermal.ceiling_hz(env.temp_cdeg);

        // --- Voltage policy ---
        let voltage_clk = self.voltage.ceiling_hz(env.voltage_mv);

        // --- Combined: most conservative wins ---
        workload_clk.min(thermal_clk).min(voltage_clk)
    }
}
```

```rust
use core::fmt;

/// Error type for clock reconfiguration.

pub enum ClockError {

    FrequencyOutOfRange,
    /// A hardware fault occurred.
    HardwareFault,

impl fmt::Display for ClockError {
    fn fmt(&self, f: &mut fmt::Formatter<'_>) -> fmt::Result {

            Self::PeripheralBusy      => write!(f, "SPI peripheral busy"),
            Self::FrequencyOutOfRange => write!(f, "Frequency out of range"),
            Self::HardwareFault       => write!(f, "Hardware fault"),

    /// exceeding it.
    ///
    /// Implementors must ensure the peripheral is idle before reconfiguring.
    ///
    /// # Returns
    /// The actual clock frequency achieved (Hz), or an error.
    fn set_clock(&mut self, target_hz: u32) -> Result<u32, ClockError>;

    /// Return the currently configured clock frequency (Hz).
    fn current_clock_hz(&self) -> u32;

    current_hz: u32,

impl DynamicSpiClock for MockSpi {
    fn set_clock(&mut self, target_hz: u32) -> Result<u32, ClockError> {
        // Find the power-of-two divisor that gives the highest achievable
        // clock without exceeding target_hz.
        let mut divisor = 2u32;
        let mut achieved = self.pclk_hz / divisor;

        while achieved > target_hz && divisor <= 256 {
            divisor *= 2;
            achieved = self.pclk_hz / divisor;
        }

        if divisor > 256 {
            return Err(ClockError::FrequencyOutOfRange);
        }

        self.current_hz = achieved;
        Ok(achieved)
    }

    fn current_clock_hz(&self) -> u32 {
        self.current_hz
    }
}
```

## Advanced Patterns

### Hysteresis to Prevent Clock Thrashing

Rapid switching between two clock rates wastes time in SPI peripheral
re-initialisation and can degrade bus stability. A simple hysteresis band
prevents oscillation:

```c
/* Apply a new clock only if it differs from the current by more than
 * HYSTERESIS_PERCENT percent. */
#define HYSTERESIS_PERCENT 15u

bool should_rescale(uint32_t current_hz, uint32_t desired_hz)
{
    uint32_t delta = (current_hz > desired_hz)
                   ? (current_hz - desired_hz)
                   : (desired_hz - current_hz);
    return (delta * 100u / current_hz) > HYSTERESIS_PERCENT;
}
```

### Clock Ramp-Up / Ramp-Down

Some slaves (especially MEMS sensors and ADCs) are sensitive to sudden
frequency changes. Stepping through intermediate frequencies avoids
glitches:

```rust
/// Ramp from `current_hz` to `target_hz` in at most `steps` steps.
pub fn ramp_clock<S: DynamicSpiClock>(
    spi: &mut S,
    target_hz: u32,
    steps: u32,
) -> Result<u32, ClockError> {
    let current = spi.current_clock_hz() as i64;
    let target  = target_hz as i64;
    let step    = (target - current) / steps as i64;

    for i in 1..=steps {
        let next = (current + step * i as i64).max(1) as u32;
        spi.set_clock(next)?;
    }
    spi.set_clock(target_hz)
}
```

### Linux Kernel / `spi-dev` Pattern

In a Linux driver, `struct spi_transfer.speed_hz` allows per-transfer
clock overrides. A userspace-visible sysfs knob can expose the policy:

```c
/* In a Linux SPI driver (simplified) */
static int my_spi_transfer(struct spi_device *spi,
                            struct spi_transfer *xfer,
                            bool low_power)
{
    xfer->speed_hz = low_power
        ? MY_SLAVE_LOW_POWER_HZ
        : MY_SLAVE_FULL_SPEED_HZ;

    /* speed_hz = 0 means "use spi_device->max_speed_hz" */
    return spi_sync_transfer(spi, xfer, 1);
}
```

## Pitfalls and Best Practices

| # | Pitfall | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Reconfiguring while BSY flag is set | Always poll/wait BSY + any inter-byte gap before changing prescaler |
| 2 | Forgetting to re-enable peripheral after disable | Wrap disable/modify/enable in a critical section or helper function |
| 3 | Clock glitch on CS assertion | De-assert CS, complete clock change, then re-assert CS |
| 4 | Ignoring slave `f_min_hz` | Some devices (e.g. SD cards, certain Flash) stop working below a minimum clock |
| 5 | Oscillating between two adjacent prescaler steps | Add hysteresis band (10–20%) around target |
| 6 | Not accounting for voltage-dependent `f_max` | Combine voltage and thermal tables in a combined-policy scaler |
| 7 | DMA buffers not flushed before re-init | Flush/invalidate caches; ensure DMA completes before peripheral disable |
| 8 | Multi-slave bus: scaling for one slave affects another | Track per-slave `f_max` and always use the minimum across active slaves |

## Summary

**SPI Dynamic Clock Scaling** is the practice of adjusting the SPI peripheral's clock frequency
at runtime rather than locking it to a single compile-time constant. The key points are:

- **Power and thermal benefits are substantial**: halving the SPI clock roughly halves the
  dynamic switching power of the peripheral and connected circuitry.

- **Three primary input signals** drive scaling decisions:
  1. *Workload* – burst vs. background / urgent vs. non-urgent transfers.
  2. *Temperature* – thermal de-rating tables cap the maximum clock as the SoC heats up.
  3. *Supply voltage* – as batteries deplete, slave `f_max` decreases; the clock must follow.

- **Implementation is straightforward** on both bare-metal (STM32 HAL, RP2040 SDK, etc.) and
  RTOS/Linux environments: disable the peripheral, update the prescaler register, re-enable.
  The entire operation takes only a few microseconds.

- **In C/C++** the common pattern is a helper function that accepts a desired clock, computes
  the optimal prescaler via a look-up or bit-scan, and writes directly to the hardware register.
  Template classes (C++) support zero-cost thermal look-up tables at compile time.

- **In Rust** the approach maps cleanly onto `no_std`-compatible structs and traits:
  a `DynamicSpiClock` trait decouples policy logic from hardware details, enabling
  test-driven development without real hardware, while integer arithmetic keeps the
  implementation dependency-free.

- **Hysteresis** and **ramp-up/ramp-down** guards prevent clock thrashing and protect
  sensitive slaves from abrupt frequency steps.

- Always respect **per-slave constraints** (`f_min_hz`, `f_max_hz`) and ensure the peripheral
  is idle (BSY clear, CS de-asserted) before any reconfiguration.

When implemented correctly, Dynamic Clock Scaling is a transparent optimisation that can
extend battery life by 10–30 % in SPI-heavy embedded applications, reduce peak junction
temperature by several degrees, and improve EMI compliance — all without sacrificing
throughput when full speed is genuinely needed.
